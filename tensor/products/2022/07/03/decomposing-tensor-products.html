Angular Momentum

The Pauli Matrices

\[J_x = \dfrac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\] \[J_y = \dfrac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix}\] \[J_z = \dfrac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\]

\(J^2 = J_x^2 + J_y^2 + J_z^2\) \(J_{\pm} = J_x \pm iJ_y\)

Commutator relation \([J_x,J_y] = 2iJ_z\)

\[J_x + iJ_y = \dfrac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\]

2 Spin Systems

2 systems, spin-\(\dfrac{1}{2}\) yields \(V_A \otimes V_B\) Working in the z-basis, we may write: \(J_{z,total} = JzA \otimes I + I \otimes JzB\)

For spin-\(\dfrac{1}{2}\) we have $$ j = 1/2, m\rangle\(and the relation\)J_+J_- = J^2 - J_z^2 + \hbar J_z$$
Applying J^2 to $$ j, m\rangle\(in general yields\)J^2 j, m\rangle = j(j+1) j, m\rangle$$
In combining these states, we can postulate which state in the four-dimensional compound state-space will have the most negative z-component of angular momentum: intuitively, it should be $$ -\rangle \otimes -\rangle$$,

Basis for 4-D Space

so that $$J^2 1/2, -1/2\rangle \otimes 1/2, -1/2\rangle\(=\)-1 1/2, -1/2\rangle \otimes 1/2, -1/2\rangle$$; -1 is an eigenvalue.

\(J^2\) and \(J_z\) both acting on the state

Hence we can write $$ 1/2, -1/2\rangle \otimes 1/2, -1/2\rangle = j = 1, m = -1\rangle.$$

To this lowest state we successively apply the raising operator \(J_+\): (include full written calculations here as well) \(J_+|1, -1\rangle = |1, 0\rangle\) which corresponds to the state \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|+-\rangle + |-+\rangle\) and applying it once more gives: \(J_+|1, 0\rangle = |1, 1\rangle\) which is the maximal \(|++\rangle\) state.

Now we have three states, but our state space is four-dimensional - we are missing one basis vector, which must also be orthogonal. Since the values for \(j = 1\) are exhausted, then we must have \(j = 0\) and hence \(m = 0\) because \(m\) is bounded by \(\pm j\).

For a fixed value of j, the range of values (and hence the dimension of the vector space) that m can take on are within \(\pm j; 2j + 1\) values.

In this decomposition, we have written the four-dimensional compound state space, i.e. the tensor product of two 2-dimensional spaces as the direct sum of a three-dimensional and one-dimensional space respectively. This is one case of the Clebsch-Gordan decomposition.
